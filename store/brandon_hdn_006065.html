Whitespine Process

At the midnight release for Words of Radiance, Isaac Stewart had a chance to sit down with Ben McSweeney to chat a little about Ben’s Shallan’s Sketchbook illustration of the whitespine. Brandon’s feedback in-line below comes from art reviews conducted through email.

Isaac: The topic of discussion would be the process behind the whitespine. Tell me what you decided to do on the first try for this.

Ben: The thing about the whitespine is that it wasn’t a creature that was clearly defined physically. It was just something that we knew everybody was afraid of, so it had to be scary and it had to be predatory and it had to have a white spine because that was in the name.

My first instinct was to go one direction, and then Brandon gave us the descriptions that were in the new text which told us that it had a snoutlike face, that it had tusks, that if you didn’t keep washing it that it would grow a shell—which was interesting, a little bit of trivia. But for the most part, we still didn’t know much about the way it looked, so that gave me a lot of room. At the same time there wasn’t a really strong idea of what it should and shouldn’t look like, so we had a bunch of iterations.

We did the one that looked kind of like a boar where the tusks were very much curved forward. We did that one first, I think. I liked that design a lot but it wasn’t what Brandon was looking for. And this is pretty standard for the design process. You try something, you show it to the boss, and he says, “Yes, no, maybe so. This one works better.”

Click to embiggen

BRANDON’S FEEDBACK:

Too many spines. Imagine in a world where an axehound is a dog—imagine a very large lion, an apex predator that’s similar but not the same. I imagine a whitespine being like an axehound but the differences between a dog and a cat, right? The crystallization thing—they can pupate and go into a dormant state, and it shouldn’t affect how they look.

The face? Too axehound-like. I’ll have to think about this one more. It’s not right. Lobster shape of body like axehound. More crablike? Let’s go a third direction, something that’s not a crab and not a lobster. Another kind of crustacean.

PETER’S FEEDBACK:

The spine that’s jammed in Rillir’s leg (or whatever) in the first book is described as a tusk. So it seems they need some kind of tusk as well.

Isaac: Brandon’s response to the first design was that we wanted to go along a different evolutionary path than the axehound, and the boar-type thing was going along the lines of the axehound in a lot of different-but-related ways.

Ben: Right, and the boar was also holding to the general arthropod/crustacean basis for the majority of the life that we’ve designed so far. So, yeah, realizing that, we had to go in a different direction. We took the tusks and we made them more like saberteeth.

Isaac: A lot of that was in response to Brandon saying, if the axehound is basically a Rosharan wolf or a dog, then maybe the whitespine is a Rosharan lion.

Click to embiggen

BRANDON’S FEEDBACK:

This is an improvement over the previous version. But it still looks like it’s based on the structure of an axehound, and we want something different. The face, tongue, and posture kind of make it look like a Rosharan hippo, and I want Rosharan velociraptor. Maybe have it walk more bipedal-like but with four legs back where a velociraptor would have two. Of course, add some kind of counterbalance where the tail would go.

The whitespine is the most dangerous non-greatshell on the planet, the most dangerous and deadly predator that you could stumble across, and I want it to look the part. It’s about the size of a bear, or slightly smaller. A whitespine does have tusks, but they don’t have to come out of the mouth like an elephant’s. Don’t think elephant at all. It’s not the image of a boar. Don’t think slow and ponderous. They could come down like sabertooth tiger tusks, or they could be chin tusks, for example.

Ben: We wanted it to be very predatory and very frightening.

Isaac: But obviously, we did not go in the cat direction entirely.

BRANDON’S FEEDBACK:

I like this direction a lot better. All of them are great. C is my favorite because it looks like it almost could be bipedal, but B is also fine. On C, I like how the tusks look like they could be hidden inside the mouth. It opens its mouth to do the “I’m going to freak you out” thing, and out come the tusks.

BRANDON’S FEEDBACK:

Head looking awesome. Body needs to be a different evolutionary track. If axehound is a lizard, this is a bird. Bipedal like a velociraptor. Actual feet needed. No huge tusks underneath, the retractable sabertooth tusks look awesome.

Ben: We wanted it to be bipedal, and so it was bipedal but at the same time almost everything has a sort of hexapedal basis. Most everything has six or eight legs or more, but we ended up going with a bipedal design which is unusual in a Roshar for anything that isn’t human. So that was interesting. And it is not so much bipedal as it is hexapedal with vestigial limbs. It has two primary limbs that drive it but you can still see the remnants of that common basis.

Ben: I think the most interesting thing about the whitespine is that we went all the way to a final design in time for delivery and then you guys were willing to go with it and I just couldn’t let it go. I don’t know if this speaks to me being precious about my role in the project or if this is just an issue of personal pride, but there was this strong sense, at least on my part, that anything that we put in the books is canon and it becomes this permanent basis for a design that many people might choose to use. Once you’ve got it in the novel, it becomes the basis for any sort of adapted work that we might create later.

So in that sense, I’m always very conscious that this is set in stone. We can’t take it back once it goes to print. We can’t say, “You know, it was a terrible design, and it doesn’t really look like that.” It’s no good. Once it is in the book, it has to look like that. Or at least we’ve established a precedent and now we have to roll with it.

That was the one where after the deadline and before the final-final drop-dead deadline, I went ahead and redesigned the creature from scratch, giving it a shinier surface that made it look more like the skin or the scales were merging into a skin shape to give it a sort of glistening, reflective surface.

We kept the white spine and we kept the face but we changed the legs, we changed the tail. We changed a lot, and I was much happier with the final design that I turned in.

Isaac: And then in this other design, you went back and said, “What are some other things that might fit into this world? Sharks?” You were thinking sharks.

Ben: Yeah, that’s right. We did go with the shark where the idea was this sort of glistening, predatory thing that tracks and hunts.

The problem was, [the first version] was just rife with fake anatomy, and I think that was the part that bothered me. It was because all the time we are making up these creatures but at the same time, I try to make an effort to study the anatomy of arthropods and crustaceans. Of animals in general to make it look plausibly real. And I can’t say I’m always successful at that, but this design looked like a guy in a suit with a fake tail and false arms, and I tried doing different things with the arms and the legs to make it feel more useful. But honestly, I realized that among other things, the segmented plates we had designed made it look way too much like a chasmfiend, and so we needed to move away from that design and do something separate. The same thing with the mandibles that I put on the face next to the tusks, they were pretty much the same mandibles the chasmfiend uses, and this is all unacceptable.

Click to embiggen

Ben: We were able to save most of the face design; I think we hit that early and Brandon enjoyed it, and I think we all agreed that the face and the head design was strong enough. But the body design absolutely had to be revised, and so I went through this entire process of revising it. I did a whole new set of roughs and we just went back through the same process where I provide three or four or five different options and we say, “I like this from option A and a little bit from option C.” We continue to do iterations again and again until we zero in on a final design and the final design is stronger on the multiple points. We have the sort of shiny wetness to the skin, and the body has more of a solid feel, like there is a skeleton in there. Like you can almost see the shape of the pelvis and the shape of the spine itself, so it feels a little bit less fake.

Click to embiggen

By having the freedom for it not to be that strictly segmented crustacean design, we were able to open up the design possibilities a little bit more, make the muscles and the skin and the surface look more evident. We lost the velociraptor claw because it was just too obviously a velociraptor claw. Changed the pincer tail into a fin tail. All these little things culminated in a final whitespine design that I feel is stronger. I’m still not sure that it’s the best of our designs in the book, but I’m certainly much more pleased with this as a design that is canonized, that will be a legacy for Words of Radiance for a long time to come.
